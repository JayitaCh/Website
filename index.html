About Me

Hi, I'm Jayita

Transportation Researcher & Data Scientist

I'm passionate about using data to tackle complex challenges in urban systems, public health, transportation, and emergency response. My work lives at the intersection of people, places, and policies, where I explore how cities function and how we can make them more equitable, resilient, and responsive. With over six years of experience, I specialize in econometric modeling, spatial analysis, and agent-based simulation. My research has spanned diverse domains—from analyzing GPS data to understand route choices among e-micromobility users, to conducting spatio-temporal studies that reveal geographic disparities in mental health service utilization. At the University of Leeds, I've led projects that inform policy and improve urban infrastructure. As a consultant, I developed discrete choice models to evaluate how safety factors influence public transportation decisions. Later, as a postdoctoral research associate, I focused on data assimilation methods to study global responses to emergencies like the COVID-19 pandemic. Looking ahead, I'm deeply committed to working on urban-related issues—whether it's improving mobility, enhancing public services, or designing data-driven interventions that make cities healthier and more inclusive.

Agent-based Simulation for COVID-19 policy response


The outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2019 prompted nations worldwide to implement various strategies to curb the rapid spread of the virus. Our analysis relies on data from Our World in Data to discern differences in the stringency index embraced by various countries. To further investigate, we categorised nations into three groups depending on the extent of measures implemented: no lockdown, partial lockdown, and full lockdown. In this regard, we developed an agent-based model to explore into the intricacies of policy adoption and its efficacy among countries worldwide.

Analysing Crime in West Yorkshire


It is evident that there are six prominent concentrations of crime incidents. These clusters represent areas where criminal activities are significantly higher compared to other regions. Analyzing the KDE values over time, it is interesting to note that the highest KDE value increases from April to September. This trend indicates that crimes are becoming more concentrated in specific locations over time, suggesting an agglomeration effect. Specifically, the cities of Leeds and Bradford show the most pronounced clusters of criminal activity, reflecting a growing density of incidents in these areas.

Simulation-based Assessment of Demand-Supply interactions for Ridesourcing services in an urban environment


The expansion of ridesourcing services without sufficient regulations have become a major concern for the urban authorities which motivates this research to assess the operational parameters of ridesourcing services. Besides, the drivers and the passengers being an integral part of ridesourcing services, various decisions adopted by both tend to influence the ridesourcing operations. The interaction among the drivers (supply) and the passengers (demand) is complex and tends to vary across time and space. So, this research develops a simulation framework to represent the dynamic mechanism of ridesourcing services. While the simulation framework is essential to assess the operational aspects of ridesourcing services, efficient urban infrastructure planning must be ensured with insights into behavioural decisions. So, this research also formulates econometric models like survival model and discrete choice model to obtain insights into the behavioural decisions of drivers and passengers.

Where do e-cargo bikes go?


Traditional bicycles can be limiting for activities like dropping kids at school, delivering goods, or spending time with family. E-cargo bikes address these limitations, encouraging users to incorporate active travel into their daily routines. These bikes also allow users to take routes they might avoid on a regular bicycle. This study aims to explore the daily routes taken by e-cargo bike users and the activities they undertake. In this regard, the participants were loaned the e-cargo bikes for one month for their household use, for free in Leeds, Brighton, and Oxford to better understand the potential to reduce car use and transport energy demand.

Factors affecting the adoption of electric buses


Although the Indian electric vehicle market is dominated by electric two-wheelers and three wheelers, various governmental schemes have boosted the inclusion of electric buses within the urban bus fleet. However, it is essential for the transport authorities to understand the acceptability of the electric buses among the commuters who are not using public transport currently. This research aims at understanding the preferences of non-public transport users, to assess the probability of them shifting their modal choice towards public transport, and electric buses in particular, within the Indian context. The results indicate that electrification of urban bus fleet can improve the image of the existing bus services thereby facilitating modal shift towards public transit among the low-income individuals who have shifted to other modes of transport due to the deterioration of the service quality of the existing transit system.

Performance Assessment of Bus Based Public Transport System


Buses form an integral part of public transport. While focus on Mass Rapid Transit System (MRTS) is equally important, lower ridership on buses will also impact ridership on MRTS. Hence, strengthening of public bus system is necessary to reduce the number of vehicles on the road, eliminating congestion, saving travel time, improving traffic safety, and reducing greenhouse gases (GHG). To assess public transport (buses) operations in the Indian Cities, a Service Level Benchmarking (SLB) framework was formulated. The framework consists of 5 dimensions and 25 measures (indicators).
